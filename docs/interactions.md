# The seven interactions, with their roots in the review

This note expands each panel and points to the part of Dolinšek, Goldschmidt and Johnson (2016) it draws from. Strain A is blue, strain B is green.

The review makes two organising distinctions that this model follows. First, whether an interaction is **passive** (the mediating molecule is an inadvertent by-product that leaks for free) or **active** (the cell pays a metabolic cost to make the molecule, so its own growth is reduced). Second, whether the interaction is **unidirectional** (one producer, one responder) or **bidirectional** (reciprocal). The review also argues that competition is pervasive: two genotypes sharing space and a substrate compete even while one helps the other, so few interactions are purely positive.

## 1. Commensalism, passive one-way positive (0, +)

A grows on substrate S and a by-product M leaks from it for free. B is a high-affinity scavenger of M (a small half-saturation, the uptake that lets a commensal live on a dilute by-product) and occupies a separate niche, so A is unaffected. This is the acetate cross-feeding picture: a specialist living on the leaked intermediate of another (review, passive unidirectional positive interactions; Rosenzweig et al. 1994; Bernstein et al. 2012).

Look for A dominating on fresh S at the front while B persists as a minor population threaded through the interior where M has accumulated.

## 2. Public good, active one-way positive (-, +)

Both strains grow on S, but A actively secretes a diffusing public good P (think of an extracellular enzyme such as a beta-lactamase) at a metabolic cost, so A grows more slowly. B exploits P without paying. The result is the cost of cooperation and the advantage of the non-producer: B is promoted while A is reduced, and B tends to overtake. This is the active side of the review's discussion of public goods and the Black Queen hypothesis (Morris, Lenski and Zinser 2012).

## 3. Facultative mutualism, passive two-way positive (+, +)

Each strain grows on S on its own and additionally leaks a by-product for free that gives the other a growth bonus. Reciprocal but facultative, so the colony still expands while staying intermixed. Both partners gain, neither pays a cost. This is the passive version of reciprocal cross-feeding.

## 4. Obligate mutualism, active two-way positive (+, +)

Engineered auxotroph-style cross-feeding. A requires a metabolite made by B and pays to make the metabolite B needs, and vice versa. Neither grows without the other close by, so the strains must stay finely intermixed and the colony is the smallest of the positive cases. The review notes that efficient reciprocal exchange opposes the genetic drift that would otherwise demix an expanding front (Müller et al. 2014) and that obligate exchange is costly and slow to start. Both signatures appear here: tight mixing and a small, slow colony.

## 5. Competition, passive two-way negative (-, -)

One shared substrate and nothing else. This is the most common negative interaction in nature, the inadvertent, passive drain each strain places on the other. At an expanding front with no other coupling, the strains demix into single-strain sectors, the classic outcome of genetic drift at a growing frontier (Hallatschek et al. 2007). The lineage view makes the sectors obvious.

## 6. Amensalism, active one-way negative (0, -)

A spends a small fraction of its growth making a diffusing antibiotic that inhibits B, and B has essentially no effect back on A. Because the toxin stays local (zero-flux boundary) it builds up around the A patches and clears a margin of B, so A gains territory and the strains segregate asymmetrically. This is the antibiotic inhibition the review groups under active negative interactions.

## 7. Predation and parasitism (-, +)

A mixed-sign interaction. Predator B grows only by contacting prey A and lyses the prey it touches; the panel starts from a prey colony with a minority of predators (the review notes the predator-prey case is the one it models in chemostat rather than batch). The predator advances as green inroads into a growing prey colony, while prey at the front can stay ahead of the predator: the spatial refuge effect. Contact predation here stands in for systems such as Bdellovibrio.

## Beyond binary interactions

The review devotes a section to assemblages of more than two strains, where the dynamics often cannot be predicted from the pairwise interactions alone. The cleanest example is rock-paper-scissors, three strains each producing a toxin that kills one of the others, which can coexist only in a spatially structured environment (Kerr et al. 2002; Kelsic et al. 2015). Adding this is the natural next exercise: a third strain and a third toxin field, with each strain inhibited by one neighbour. The engine here already supports arbitrary numbers of fields, so the change is contained to a new interaction class.

## Why spatial structure matters here

Several of the review's central claims are only visible because the model is spatially explicit:

- Cross-feeding, public-good and toxin effects depend on local concentration, which only builds up when diffusion does not wash secreted molecules into a well-mixed bulk.
- The cost of an active interaction shows up spatially: the public-good producer loses ground to the non-producer, and the obligate mutualists stay mixed and small.
- Competitors demix into sectors, mutualists stay mixed, and predator and prey separate in space with a front refuge for the prey.

A well-mixed model would collapse all of these to mean-field abundances and lose the spatial signatures that distinguish the interactions.
